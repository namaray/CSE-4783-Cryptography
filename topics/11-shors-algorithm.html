1. What is Shor's Algorithm?

Shor's Algorithm is a quantum computer algorithm formulated by Peter Shor in 1994. It is designed to find the prime factors of an integer \( N \).

While classical computers take sub-exponential time to factor large numbers (using algorithms like the General Number Field Sieve), Shor's algorithm runs in polynomial time on a quantum computer. This means it is exponentially faster than the most efficient known classical factoring algorithm.

2. The Threat to Cryptography

Most modern public-key cryptography relies on mathematical problems that are easy to compute in one direction but extremely difficult to reverse on classical computers.

  • RSA Encryption: Relies on the difficulty of integer factorization (finding \( p \) and \( q \) from \( N \)).
  • Diffie-Hellman & DSA/ECDSA: Relies on the difficulty of solving the Discrete Logarithm Problem (DLP).
🚨 The Quantum Threat: Shor's Algorithm can solve BOTH integer factorization and the discrete logarithm problem in polynomial time. If a sufficiently large, stable quantum computer is ever built, RSA, Diffie-Hellman, and Elliptic Curve Cryptography will be completely broken.

This threat has sparked the massive transition towards Post-Quantum Cryptography (PQC), which involves mathematical problems (like lattice-based cryptography) that even quantum computers cannot solve efficiently.

3. How Shor's Algorithm Works (High-Level)

The algorithm consists of two main parts: a classical reduction step and a quantum period-finding step.

Part 1: Classical Reduction

The factorization problem is reduced to the problem of finding the period of a function.

  1. Choose a random number \( a < N \).
  2. Compute the greatest common divisor \( \gcd(a, N) \). If it is greater than 1, we got extremely lucky and have already found a non-trivial factor of \( N \). We are done.
  3. If \( \gcd(a, N) = 1 \), then \( a \) and \( N \) are coprime. Proceed to the quantum step.

Part 2: Quantum Period Finding

We need to find the period \( r \) of the modular exponentiation function:

\( f(x) = a^x \bmod N \)

The period \( r \) is the smallest positive integer such that \( a^r \equiv 1 \pmod N \). Finding \( r \) classically is too slow, but a quantum computer can find it quickly using a Quantum Fourier Transform (QFT).

Part 3: Classical Factor Extraction

Once the quantum computer finds the period \( r \):

  1. If \( r \) is odd, the algorithm fails. Go back to step 1 and pick a new \( a \).
  2. If \( a^{r/2} \equiv -1 \pmod N \), the algorithm fails. Pick a new \( a \).
  3. Otherwise, both \( (a^{r/2} - 1) \) and \( (a^{r/2} + 1) \) share a factor with \( N \).

We can compute the prime factors of \( N \) using the classical Euclidean algorithm:

Factor 1 = \( \gcd(a^{r/2} + 1, N) \)
Factor 2 = \( \gcd(a^{r/2} - 1, N) \)
