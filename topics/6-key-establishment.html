1. The Key Distribution Problem

For two parties to communicate securely using symmetric cryptography, they first need to agree on a shared secret key. But how can they securely exchange this key over an insecure channel — without an adversary intercepting it?

  • Key Distribution: Getting a key from the generator to the user(s) securely.
  • Key Agreement: A protocol by which two or more parties jointly establish a shared secret without either party choosing the key alone.
  • Key Transport: One party generates a key and securely sends it to another party.
⚠️ Problem with Symmetric Key Distribution: For n users, you need \( \frac{n(n-1)}{2} \) unique keys for all pairs to communicate privately. This becomes unmanageable for large networks.

2. Diffie-Hellman Key Exchange (DHKE)

Proposed by Whitfield Diffie and Martin Hellman in 1976. The first practical protocol for establishing a shared secret over a public channel. It is based on the computational difficulty of the Discrete Logarithm Problem.

Setup (Public Parameters)

  • A large prime number \( p \)
  • A generator \( g \) (a primitive root modulo \( p \)), where \( 1 < g < p \)

Protocol Steps

  1. Alice chooses a secret random integer \( a \) and computes \( A = g^a \pmod{p} \). She sends \( A \) to Bob.
  2. Bob chooses a secret random integer \( b \) and computes \( B = g^b \pmod{p} \). He sends \( B \) to Alice.
  3. Alice computes the shared secret: \( K = B^a \pmod{p} = g^{ba} \pmod{p} \)
  4. Bob computes the shared secret: \( K = A^b \pmod{p} = g^{ab} \pmod{p} \)
  5. Both arrive at the same shared key \( K \) since \( g^{ab} = g^{ba} \).
Public: \( p, g \)     Alice's secret: \( a \)     Bob's secret: \( b \)

Shared Key: \( K = g^{ab} \pmod{p} \)

Security: The Discrete Logarithm Problem

An eavesdropper Eve sees \( p, g, A = g^a \pmod p, B = g^b \pmod p \). To find the shared secret, she must compute either \( a \) or \( b \) from \( A \) or \( B \), which is the Discrete Logarithm Problem. No efficient algorithm is known for large primes.

🚨 Vulnerability: Man-in-the-Middle (MITM) Attack!
DHKE does not provide authentication. Eve can intercept the exchange, pose as Bob to Alice (establishing key \( K_1 = g^{ae} \)) and pose as Alice to Bob (establishing key \( K_2 = g^{be} \)), reading and re-encrypting all traffic. DHKE must be combined with authentication (e.g., digital signatures) to be secure.

3. Key Distribution Center (KDC)

A trusted third party that helps manage key distribution in a network with many users. Each user shares a unique master key with the KDC.

Needham-Schroeder Protocol (Simplified)

When Alice wants to communicate with Bob:

  1. Alice sends her ID, Bob's ID, and a nonce \( N_A \) to the KDC.
  2. KDC generates a session key \( K_{AB} \) and sends Alice: \( E_{K_A}(K_{AB}, N_A, B, E_{K_B}(K_{AB}, A)) \). The portion encrypted with Bob's master key is a "ticket".
  3. Alice decrypts her portion to get \( K_{AB} \) and the ticket. She forwards the ticket to Bob.
  4. Bob decrypts the ticket with his master key \( K_B \) to get \( K_{AB} \) and Alice's identity.
  5. Alice and Bob now share \( K_{AB} \) and can communicate securely.
⚠️ KDC Limitations: Single point of failure. If the KDC is compromised, all communications are exposed. Requires a secure channel to establish master keys with all users.

4. Public Key Infrastructure (PKI) & Certificates

Public key cryptography solves the key distribution problem — anyone can use Alice's public key. But how do you verify the public key you received really belongs to Alice and not an attacker?

Digital Certificates

A digital certificate is a document that binds a public key to an identity (name, organization, etc.). It is digitally signed by a trusted Certificate Authority (CA).

  • Certificate Contents: Subject name, Subject's public key, Issuer (CA) name, Validity period, CA's digital signature.
  • X.509 Standard: The most widely used certificate format (used in HTTPS/TLS).

Chain of Trust

CAs form a hierarchy. A Root CA signs certificates for Intermediate CAs, which sign certificates for end entities. Browsers and OS pre-install Root CA certificates as "trust anchors."

💡 Certificate Revocation: If a private key is compromised, the corresponding certificate can be revoked. This is managed via Certificate Revocation Lists (CRL) or OCSP (Online Certificate Status Protocol).

5. Elliptic Curve Diffie-Hellman (ECDH)

A variant of DHKE using Elliptic Curve Cryptography (ECC). ECC provides the same security as RSA/DHKE but with much smaller key sizes, making it ideal for mobile and constrained devices.

The security of ECC is based on the Elliptic Curve Discrete Logarithm Problem (ECDLP), which is considered harder to solve than the standard discrete logarithm problem for the same key size.
