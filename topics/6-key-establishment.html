DHKE, the Man-in-the-Middle Attack, and DHKE with Certificates

1. The Key Distribution Problem

For symmetric crypto, \( n \) users communicating pairwise need:

\( \frac{n(n-1)}{2} \) unique secret keys

This becomes unmanageable at scale. Public-key / asymmetric cryptography solves this — anyone can encrypt to Alice using her public key, but only Alice can decrypt. The remaining challenge: how do you securely distribute and authenticate public keys?

2. Diffie-Hellman Key Exchange (DHKE) — Basics

DHKE lets two parties agree on a shared secret over a public channel. Based on the Discrete Logarithm Problem.

Public Parameters (known to everyone)

  • \( p \) — a large prime (e.g., \( p > 2^{1024} \))
  • \( \alpha \) — a primitive root (generator) modulo \( p \)

Protocol

Alice
Public Channel
Bob
Chooses secret \( a \) (private key)
Computes \( A = \alpha^a \bmod p \)
A ──────────→
Chooses secret \( b \) (private key)
Computes \( B = \alpha^b \bmod p \)
Receives \( B \)
Computes \( k_{AB} = B^a \bmod p = \alpha^{ba} \bmod p \)
←────────── B
Sends \( B \), receives \( A \)
Computes \( k_{AB} = A^b \bmod p = \alpha^{ab} \bmod p \)
Shared Secret: \( k_{AB} = \alpha^{ab} \bmod p \)    (same for both, since \( \alpha^{ab} = \alpha^{ba} \))
💡 Security basis: An eavesdropper sees \( p, \alpha, A = \alpha^a \bmod p, B = \alpha^b \bmod p \). Computing \( a \) or \( b \) from these is the Discrete Logarithm Problem — computationally infeasible for large \( p \).

If parameters are carefully chosen (especially \( p > 2^{1024} \)), DHKE is secure against passive (listen-only) attacks.

3. The Man-in-the-Middle (MIM) Attack

DHKE is not authenticated — it is completely broken against an active attacker (Oscar) who can intercept and modify messages. This is the most important vulnerability to understand.

How the Attack Works

Alice
Oscar (Attacker)
Bob
Sends \( A = \alpha^a \bmod p \)
A ──→ intercepts ──→ A′ Oscar sends his own \( A' = \alpha^o \bmod p \) to Bob
Receives \( A' \) (thinks it's from Alice)
Receives \( B' \) (thinks it's from Bob)
B′ ←── intercepts ←── B Oscar sends his own \( B' = \alpha^o \bmod p \) to Alice
Sends \( B = \alpha^b \bmod p \)
Computes \( k_{AO} = (B')^a = \alpha^{oa} \)
Knows both keys:
\( k_{AO} = A^o = \alpha^{ao} \)
\( k_{BO} = B^o = \alpha^{bo} \)
Computes \( k_{BO} = (A')^b = \alpha^{ob} \)
🚨 Result: Oscar now holds two separate session keys — one with Alice (\( k_{AO} \)) and one with Bob (\( k_{BO} \)). He can read all messages, decrypt them with one key, and re-encrypt with the other. Alice and Bob believe they are talking to each other.

Why the Attack Works — Root Cause

Alice computes: \( k_{AO} = (B')^a = (\alpha^o)^a \)
Oscar computes: \( k_{AO} = A^o = (\alpha^a)^o \)    ✓ same key

Bob computes:    \( k_{BO} = (A')^b = (\alpha^o)^b \)
Oscar computes: \( k_{BO} = B^o = (\alpha^b)^o \)    ✓ same key
📌 Underlying Problem: Public keys are not authenticated. When Alice receives what is supposedly Bob's public key, she has no way to verify it really belongs to Bob — the bits have no identity attached.

The MIM attack is not restricted to DHKE. It applies to any public-key scheme (RSA, ECDSA, etc.) by the same pattern: Oscar replaces a party's public key with his own.

4. Certificates — The Solution

To prevent the MIM attack, all public keys must be digitally signed by a trusted third party. This signed binding is called a Certificate.

What is a Certificate?

\( \text{Certificate} = \text{public key} + \text{ID(user)} + \text{digital signature over public key and ID} \)

In formal notation, Alice's certificate issued by a Certificate Authority (CA) is:

\( \text{Cert}(\text{Alice}) = \left( k_{\text{pub}},\; \text{ID}(\text{Alice}),\; \text{sig}_{K_{CA}}\!\left(k_{\text{pub}},\; \text{ID}(\text{Alice})\right) \right) \)
📜 Example Certificate for Alice
SubjectAlice (ID = alice@example.com)
Subject's Public Key\( k_{\text{pub,Alice}} = \alpha^a \bmod p \)
Issuer (CA)Trusted Certificate Authority
Validity Period2024-01-01 to 2025-01-01
CA's Signature\( \text{sig}_{K_{CA}}(k_{\text{pub,Alice}},\; \text{ID}(\text{Alice})) \)
  • The CA signs the certificate with its private key \( k_{CA} \).
  • Anyone who trusts the CA can verify the certificate using the CA's public key \( K_{\text{pub,CA}} \).
  • Certificates bind the identity of a user to their public key — breaking the link that the MIM attack exploits.

5. DHKE with Certificates ⭐ (Main Topic)

The authenticated version of DHKE. Both Alice and Bob obtain certificates from the CA in advance. These certificates are then exchanged during the key exchange to authenticate each other's DH public key.

Pre-Setup (done once, before the protocol)

  • Alice's cert: \( \text{Cert}(\text{Alice}) = ((A,\, \text{ID}_A),\; \text{sig}_{K_{CA}}(A, \text{ID}_A)) \) where \( A = \alpha^a \bmod p \)
  • Bob's cert: \( \text{Cert}(\text{Bob}) = ((B,\, \text{ID}_B),\; \text{sig}_{K_{CA}}(B, \text{ID}_B)) \) where \( B = \alpha^b \bmod p \)
  • Both Alice and Bob already know the CA's public key \( K_{\text{pub,CA}} \)

Protocol Execution

Alice
Public Channel
Bob
Sends her certificate
Cert(Alice) ──────→
Receives Cert(Alice)
Receives Cert(Bob)
←────── Cert(Bob)
Sends his certificate
Verifies Bob's cert:
\( \text{ver}_{K_{\text{pub,CA}}}(\text{Cert}(\text{Bob})) \)
✅ If valid: extracts \( B \)
Computes \( k_{AB} = B^a \bmod p \)
—
Verifies Alice's cert:
\( \text{ver}_{K_{\text{pub,CA}}}(\text{Cert}(\text{Alice})) \)
✅ If valid: extracts \( A \)
Computes \( k_{AB} = A^b \bmod p \)
Shared secret: \( k_{AB} = \alpha^{ab} \bmod p \) — established securely and with mutual authentication
✅ Why MIM fails now: Oscar cannot forge certificates because he does not have the CA's private key \( k_{CA} \). If Oscar tries to substitute his own public key, the certificate verification step will fail — Alice and Bob will detect the tampering and abort.

Why This is Better Than Exchanging Raw Public Keys

Q: Haven't we just moved the problem? We still need to authenticate the CA's public key \( K_{\text{pub,CA}} \).

A: Yes, but only once. The CA's public key is distributed once at system setup — browsers, for example, are shipped with the CA public keys pre-installed. The authenticated channel is the (hopefully) correct distribution of the original software. This is dramatically more scalable than authenticating every individual public key.

6. Public-Key Infrastructure (PKI)

Definition: The entire system formed by Certificate Authorities together with the necessary support mechanisms is called a Public-Key Infrastructure (PKI).

X.509 Certificate Standard

In real-world systems, certificates contain much more than just a public key and signature. The X.509 standard is the most widely used certificate format (used in HTTPS/TLS). Key fields:

FieldContents
VersionCertificate format version (usually v3)
Serial NumberUnique number per certificate issued by this CA
SubjectIdentity of the certificate owner (name, organization, domain)
Subject's Public KeyThe actual public key being certified (DH, RSA, ECDSA key)
Issuer (CA)Name of the Certificate Authority that signed this
Validity PeriodNot Before / Not After dates
Signature AlgorithmAlgorithm used by CA to sign (e.g., SHA256withRSA)
CA's SignatureDigital signature over all other fields (after hashing). This is what binds everything together.
⚠️ Two Public-Key Schemes in Every Certificate:
  1. The subject's public key — what is being protected (e.g., the DH key \( A = \alpha^a \bmod p \))
  2. The CA's signature algorithm — the separate scheme used to sign the certificate data itself (e.g., RSA or ECDSA)

7. Chain of Trust & Certificate Revocation

Chain of Trust (Cross-Certification)

In large systems, users may hold certificates from different CAs. This is handled by cross-certification:

  • CA1 certifies the public key of CA2 — if Alice trusts CA1, she also trusts CA2 (trust is delegated).
  • This forms a hierarchy: Root CAs → Intermediate CAs → End-user certificates.
  • Web browsers ship with Root CA public keys pre-installed as trust anchors.

Certificate Revocation Lists (CRLs)

Certificates must sometimes be revoked before their expiry date — e.g., if a smart card is lost, a private key is compromised, or a user leaves an organization.

  • CAs publish Certificate Revocation Lists (CRLs) periodically (e.g., daily).
  • Any party verifying a certificate should check against the current CRL.
  • CRL distribution places a bandwidth burden on the PKI system.
  • Modern alternative: OCSP (Online Certificate Status Protocol) — on-demand real-time revocation checking.
💡 Remaining PKI Challenges: Even with certificates and CRLs, distributing CA public keys securely, managing cross-certifications, and handling revocation at scale remain active engineering problems in real-world systems.
