Part I — Zero-Knowledge Proofs (ZKP)

A Zero-Knowledge Proof is a cryptographic protocol where one party (the Prover P) can convince another party (the Verifier V) that they know a secret, without revealing anything about the secret itself.

💡 The Core Idea: "I can prove to you I know the password to this locked door, without ever showing you the password or unlocking the door in front of you."

Ali Baba Cave Analogy

Imagine a circular cave with a secret door inside that requires a password to open. Paths split into left and right. The Prover (P) knows the password:

  1. The Verifier waits at the cave entrance. The Prover enters and goes either left or right (Verifier doesn't see which).
  2. The Verifier calls out a random direction — "Come out from the left!" or "Come out from the right!"
  3. If the Prover knows the password, they can always come out from the demanded side. If they don't know the password, they can only succeed 50% of the time (by luck).
  4. Repeating this \( n \) times, the probability of a cheating prover succeeding is \( (1/2)^n \). For \( n = 100 \) rounds, this probability is \( 2^{-100} \), effectively zero.
Probability of cheating prover succeeding \( n \) rounds: \( \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^n \xrightarrow{n \to \infty} 0 \)

ZKP — Three Essential Properties

✅

Completeness

If the statement is true, an honest verifier will always be convinced by an honest prover.

🔒

Soundness

If the statement is false, no cheating prover can convince the honest verifier that it is true (except with negligible probability).

🕵️

Zero-Knowledge

If the statement is true, the verifier learns nothing other than the fact that the statement is true. No information about the secret is revealed.

Part II — Schnorr's Identification Protocol

Claus-Peter Schnorr's protocol is a practical, efficient interactive ZKP. It allows Alice (Prover) to prove to Bob (Verifier) that she knows a private key \( x \), without revealing \( x \). It is based on the Discrete Logarithm Problem.

Setup & Keys

🔵 Alice (Prover)

  • Public prime generator: \( G \)
  • Private key: \( x \) (secret)
  • Public key: \( P = x \cdot G \)

🔴 Bob (Verifier)

  • Knows the prime generator \( G \)
  • Knows Alice's public key \( P \)
  • Does NOT know \( x \)

Protocol Steps (3-Round Interactive Proof)

  1. Alice generates a random nonce \( r \) (kept secret).
  2. Alice computes the commitment \( R = r \cdot G \) and sends \( R \) to Bob.
  3. Bob sends a random challenge \( c \) to Alice.
  4. Alice computes the response \( s = c \cdot x + r \) and sends \( (R, s) \) to Bob.
  5. Bob verifies: He checks if \( s \cdot G = c \cdot P + R \).

Mathematical Proof of Correctness

Bob checks: \( s \cdot G \stackrel{?}{=} (c \cdot x + r) \cdot G \)

Expanding: \( s \cdot G = c \cdot (x \cdot G) + r \cdot G = c \cdot P + R \) ✓

📝 Original handwritten notes

✅ Why it's Zero-Knowledge: Bob learns \( R = r \cdot G \) and \( s = c \cdot x + r \). To find \( x \), Bob would need to compute \( r = s - c \cdot x \), but he doesn't know \( x \). The Discrete Log Problem prevents deriving \( r \) from \( R \). Alice's secret \( x \) is perfectly protected.
⚠️ Nonce reuse attack: If Alice reuses the same nonce \( r \) for two different challenges \( c_1 \) and \( c_2 \), Bob gets two responses: \( s_1 = c_1 x + r \) and \( s_2 = c_2 x + r \). Subtracting gives \( s_1 - s_2 = (c_1 - c_2)x \), revealing \( x = \frac{s_1 - s_2}{c_1 - c_2} \). The nonce must ALWAYS be freshly random!

Part III — Distributed Key Generation (DKG)

DKG is a protocol that allows multiple parties to jointly generate a shared secret key such that no single party knows the full key. It extends Secret Sharing schemes (like Shamir's Secret Sharing) to the distributed setting.

The Problem it Solves

In traditional key generation, a single trusted party generates the key. This creates a single point of failure. DKG eliminates this by distributing the trust across multiple parties.

Shamir's Secret Sharing (Foundation of DKG)

A secret \( S \) is split into \( n \) shares such that any \( t \) shares can reconstruct \( S \), but fewer than \( t \) shares reveal nothing. This is called a \( (t, n) \)-threshold scheme.

  • Choose a random polynomial \( f(x) \) of degree \( t-1 \) where \( f(0) = S \) (the secret).
  • Each party \( i \) receives share \( f(i) \).
  • Reconstruct using Lagrange Interpolation.

Lagrange Interpolation (Reconstruction)

\( f(0) = \sum_{i} y_i \cdot L_i(0) \) where \( L_i(0) = \prod_{j \neq i} \frac{0 - x_j}{x_i - x_j} \pmod{p} \)

Worked Example from Notes

Three users contribute shares: \( A(1, 235) \), \( B(2, 230) \), \( C(3, 108) \). Modulus: 251.

The secret polynomial is \( f(x) = S + ax + bx^2 \), and \( f(0) = S \) is our goal.

Compute Lagrange basis polynomials at \( x = 0 \):

\( L_1(0) = \frac{0 - x_2}{x_1 - x_2} \times \frac{0 - x_3}{x_1 - x_3} = \frac{-2}{1-2} \times \frac{-3}{1-3} = \frac{-2}{-1} \times \frac{-3}{-2} = 2 \times \frac{3}{2} = 3 \pmod{251} \)
\( L_2(0) = \frac{0 - x_1}{x_2 - x_1} \times \frac{0 - x_3}{x_2 - x_3} = \frac{0-1}{2-1} \times \frac{0-3}{2-3} = \frac{-1}{1} \times \frac{-3}{-1} = -3 \pmod{251} = 248 \)
\( L_3(0) = \frac{0-1}{3-1} \times \frac{0-2}{3-2} = \frac{-1}{2} \times \frac{-2}{1} = 1 \pmod{251} \)
\( f(0) = y_1 \cdot L_1(0) + y_2 \cdot L_2(0) + y_3 \cdot L_3(0) \)
\( = 235 \times 3 + 230 \times (-3) + 108 \times 1 \pmod{251} \)
\( = 705 - 690 + 108 = 123 \pmod{251} \)
Secret \( S = f(0) = 123 \)

📝 Original handwritten notes

💡 DKG in Distributed Systems: In a full DKG protocol (e.g., Pedersen DKG), each party contributes a random polynomial. The shared secret is the sum of all parties' constant terms (secrets), but no party needs to reveal their individual secret to compute this sum.
