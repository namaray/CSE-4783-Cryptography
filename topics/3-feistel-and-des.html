1. The Feistel Cipher

The plaintext is divided into two equal-length blocks: Left (\( L_p \)) and Right (\( R_p \)). During encryption, a non-invertible function \( f \) takes the key \( K \) and \( R_p \) as input. The output is XOR-ed with \( L_p \).

Problems with Basic Feistel

In the basic model, the right block of the plaintext never changes and remains the same in the ciphertext. This makes it highly vulnerable to attacks.

Enhancements

  • Multiple Rounds: The number of rounds was increased.
  • Swapper: A swapper element was added to swap the left and right blocks after every round.
  • Multiple Keys: Different round keys (\( K_1, K_2, ... \)) are used. Decryption uses these keys in reverse order.
Mathematical Proof of Decryption (with Swapper):
\( L'_p = R'_c \oplus f(L'_c, K) \)
Since \( R'_c = L_p \oplus f(R_p, K) \) and \( L'_c = R_p \):
\( L'_p = L_p \oplus f(R_p, K) \oplus f(R_p, K) \)
\( L'_p = L_p \oplus 0 = L_p \) (Using XOR identity \( X \oplus X = 0 \))

2. Data Encryption Standard (DES)

Adopted by NIST in 1977, DES is the most widely used block cipher in the world. It is based directly on the enhanced Feistel cipher structure.

  • Block Size: 64 bits
  • Key Size: 56 bits (originally 64 bits, but 8 are parity bits)
  • Rounds: 16 rounds of processing
  • Goal: Achieve a high degree of Shannon's Diffusion and Confusion.

General Structure

  1. Initial Permutation (IP): 64-bit plaintext is permuted.
  2. 16 Rounds: Feistel network using the \( F \) function and 16 distinct 48-bit round keys.
  3. 32-bit Swap: Left and right halves are swapped.
  4. Final Permutation (Inverse IP): The inverse of the initial permutation creates the 64-bit ciphertext.

3. The DES \( F \) Function

The core of DES is the \( F \) function. It takes a 32-bit Right-half input (\( R \)) and a 48-bit Round Key (\( K \)) and produces a 32-bit output.

\( F(R, K) = P(S(E(R) \oplus K)) \)
  • \( E \) Expansion P-box: Expands 32 bits to 48 bits.
  • XOR Operation: 48 bits from the E-box are XORed with the 48-bit Round Key \( K_i \).
  • \( S \)-boxes: Shrinks 48 bits back to 32 bits.
  • \( P \) Straight Permutation: Permutes the resulting 32 bits.

S-Boxes (Substitution Boxes)

There are 8 distinct S-boxes. Each takes a 6-bit input and produces a 4-bit output.

Selection Rule: For a 6-bit input b1 b2 b3 b4 b5 b6

  • Row Selection: The outer bits (1 and 6) combine to select one of 4 rows (00, 01, 10, 11).
  • Column Selection: The inner 4 bits (2, 3, 4, 5) select the column (0 to 15).

Example: Input to \( S_1 \) is 101010. Outer bits are 10 (Row 2). Inner bits are 0101 (Column 5). The value at Row 2, Column 5 is 6 (0110 in binary).

4. DES Key Generation

Produces sixteen 48-bit keys out of the original 56-bit cipher key. One for each round.

  1. Parity Drop (PC-1): A compression transposition step. Drops parity bits at positions 8, 16, 24, 32, 40, 48, 56, and 64, leaving 56 bits.
  2. Split: Divides the 56 bits into two halves of 28 bits each.
  3. Circular Left Shift: Shifts each half by 1 or 2 bits depending on the round.
    • Rounds 1, 2, 9, 16: Shifting is done by 1 bit.
    • Other Rounds: Shifting is done by 2 bits.
  4. Compression P-box (PC-2): Compresses the 56-bit input to produce a 48-bit round key.

5. Avalanche Effect & Attacks on DES

Avalanche effect is a desirable property where a small change in the plaintext or key results in a significant change in the ciphertext (evidence of high diffusion/confusion).

  • Changing 1 bit in plaintext affects ~34 bits in ciphertext.
  • Changing 1 bit in the key affects ~35 bits in ciphertext.

Known Attacks

  • Brute-Force: Due to a key space of \( 2^{56} \approx 7.2 \times 10^{16} \), it was thought secure. However, the $250k DES Cracker found a key in 56 hours in 1999 (and later, in 22 hours). DES is now considered insecure.
  • Differential Cryptanalysis: Can find a key with \( 2^{47} \) plaintext-ciphertext pairs.
  • Linear Cryptanalysis: Can find a key with \( 2^{43} \) plaintext-ciphertext pairs.

6. Multiple Encryption (2DES & 3DES)

To overcome DES's short key length without designing a whole new algorithm, multiple encryptions were proposed.

2DES & The Meet-In-The-Middle Attack

2DES Encryption: \( C = E_{K2}(E_{K1}(P)) \)
Key length: \( 56 \times 2 = 112 \) bits

Vulnerability: 2DES is broken by the Meet-in-the-Middle Attack. Given a known pair (P, C):

  1. Encrypt P with all \( 2^{56} \) possible keys for K1.
  2. Decrypt C with all \( 2^{56} \) possible keys for K2.
  3. If the intermediate results match, try the keys on another pair.
  4. Takes \( \approx 2^{57} \) steps, not much harder than cracking 1-DES.

3DES (Triple DES)

Uses an Encrypt-Decrypt-Encrypt (EDE) sequence.

3DES with 2 Keys: \( C = E_{K1}(D_{K2}(E_{K1}(M))) \)
3DES with 3 Keys: \( C = E_{K3}(D_{K2}(E_{K1}(M))) \)

Backward Compatibility: If \( K_1 = K_2 \), 3DES acts just like regular 1-DES (Encrypts, decrypts to original, encrypts again). Standardized in ANSI X9.17 & ISO 8732.

Note: Effective key length of 3-key 3DES is 112 bits, not 168 bits, due to a variation of the meet-in-the-middle attack.
